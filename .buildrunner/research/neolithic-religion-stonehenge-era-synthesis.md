# Neolithic Religion of Britain and Surrounding Areas, c. 4000–2200 BCE

**A synthesis of multi-agent archaeological research on the religion and ritual life of the people who built Stonehenge.**

_Compiled: 2026-04-25. Sources: six parallel Claude research agents (monuments, burial, cosmology, artifacts, astronomy, society) plus one Codex research agent (environment, water, rock art, altered states, recent discoveries)._

---

## 0. Prefatory Caveats

Neolithic Britain and Ireland left no writing. Everything that follows is reconstructed from monuments, deposits, faunal remains, ancient DNA, isotopes, carved symbols, and landscape patterns, interpreted through archaeology, archaeoastronomy, anthropology, and increasingly bioarchaeology. Throughout this document, three epistemic tiers are distinguished:

- **Evidence** — directly observed in the archaeological record.
- **Inference** — well-supported interpretation of that evidence.
- **Speculation** — plausible but weakly supported hypothesis.

The single most important misconception to dispatch before going further: **the Druids had nothing to do with Stonehenge or any Neolithic monument.** Druids appear in classical sources from the 4th–1st centuries BCE — Iron Age, at least 2,000 years after Stonehenge's sarsen phase. The association was invented by 17th–18th-century antiquarians (Aubrey, Stukeley) and survives only in modern neo-Druidic ceremony, which is an 18th-century revival with no genealogical link to anything prehistoric.

## 1. Chronological and Demographic Framework

### 1.1 The arrival of farming, c. 4000 BCE

Britain's Neolithic begins with the arrival of farming around 4000 BCE. Ancient DNA work by **Selina Brace, Thomas Booth, David Reich and colleagues** (_Nature Ecology & Evolution_, 2019) sequenced 6 Mesolithic and 67 Neolithic individuals and showed the transition was driven by **migration**, not local adoption. The incoming Neolithic farmers were genetically closest to **Iberian Neolithic populations**, themselves descended from Aegean farmers who reached Britain via a Mediterranean-Atlantic route. Mesolithic hunter-gatherer ancestry was largely submerged.

These farmers brought with them more than seed and stock. They brought a religious package whose continental roots lay in the **Linearbandkeramik (LBK)**, **Michelsberg**, and especially **Funnel Beaker (TRB)** traditions — cultures already building causewayed enclosures, megalithic tombs, and monumental landscapes across northern and Atlantic Europe.

### 1.2 The Beaker replacement, c. 2400 BCE

A second demographic event — the Bell Beaker arrival — closes the period. **Olalde, Reich et al.** (_Nature_, 2018) showed that incoming Beaker populations from the Lower Rhine (genetically closest to individuals at Oostwoud, Netherlands) carried high steppe-related ancestry and, within a few centuries, replaced approximately **90% of Britain's Neolithic gene pool**. With them came individual inhumation under round barrows, copper metallurgy, archery kit, and a religion organized around personal identity rather than collective ancestry. The communal monument tradition was the work of the Anatolian-derived farmers, and was largely extinguished as a living religion shortly after Stonehenge's sarsen phase was completed.

### 1.3 Phases within the British/Irish Neolithic

- **Early Neolithic (c. 4000–3400 BCE):** First farms, causewayed enclosures, Carinated Bowl pottery, long barrows, court cairns, the first portal tombs.
- **Middle Neolithic (c. 3400–3000 BCE):** Cursus monuments, passage tomb florescence (Boyne Valley, Orkney), Peterborough Wares.
- **Late Neolithic (c. 3000–2400 BCE):** Henges, stone circles, Grooved Ware, the Stonehenge sarsen phase, the Ness of Brodgar, Durrington Walls, mass pilgrimage feasting.
- **Chalcolithic transition (c. 2400–2200 BCE):** Bell Beaker arrival, individual round-barrow burial, the Amesbury Archer, the cultural disruption of the older system.

## 2. Environmental and Economic Religion

The agricultural revolution did not simply change diet — it sacralized the **domesticated cycle**. Religion became increasingly tied to clearing, planting, herding, milking, slaughtering, and gathering at fixed seasonal moments.

**Pollen and archaeobotanical evidence** (O'Connell and Molloy on Ireland; McClatchie, Bogaard, Treasure, Grocke, Caseldine, and Church on Britain) shows woodland clearance, then patchy regeneration, with **emmer wheat** dominant and **barley** widespread. The "first fields" of **Céide Fields** (County Mayo, traditionally c. 3500 BCE) are walled, monumental, and integrated with tombs — cultivation as territorial and ancestral claim, not just subsistence.

**Lipid residue analysis** (Richard Evershed and colleagues) demonstrated widespread **dairying** in British Neolithic pottery. Cattle and sheep were renewable producers; pigs were the periodic feast animal slaughtered at large gatherings. The religious significance of each species tracks its position in the farming year.

**Sacred groves** as such cannot be proven, but woodland retained ritual importance through **timber monuments**: Woodhenge (c. 2500 BCE), aligned to summer solstice sunrise; the timber circles inside Durrington Walls, aligned to midwinter solstice sunrise. Trees from managed woodland were transformed into ceremonial architecture that structured sacred time.

**Seasonal slaughter** at Durrington Walls, dated by tooth eruption to mid-winter (Wright, Viner-Daniels, Albarella, Parker Pearson 2014), confirms the midwinter solstice as a major ritual moment. Recent isotope work by Eric Guiry, Fiona Beglane, Neil Carlin and colleagues (2025, _Proceedings of the Prehistoric Society_) shows that Newgrange feasting c. 2600–2450 BCE focused on pigs fattened by **woodland pannage** and probably tied to the winter solstice — another convergence of agriculture, woodland, feast, and sacred calendar.

## 3. Sacred Landscape and Monumental Architecture

### 3.1 Causewayed enclosures (c. 3800–3400 BCE)

The earliest large communal monuments. **Windmill Hill** (Wiltshire, ~3700–3500 BCE, 8.5 ha, three concentric interrupted ditches) was excavated by Keiller and reassessed by Whittle. **Hambledon Hill** (Dorset, excavated by Roger Mercer 1974–1986) yielded skull fragments and long bones in ditch contexts, with evidence for episodes of fire and arrowhead-related violence. Both sites involved feasting, the curation of the dead (probable excarnation), and structured deposition.

**Etton** (Cambridgeshire, Francis Pryor, with reanalysis by Beadsmoore, Garrow, and Knight) shows a causewayed enclosure built on a floodplain island in a former meander of the Welland — explicitly liminal placement at the boundary of land and water. Deposition there was intermittent, focused, and structured.

### 3.2 Cursus monuments (c. 3600–3000 BCE)

Long, narrow parallel-ditched enclosures up to ~10 km long. Around 100 are known. The **Greater Stonehenge Cursus** (3 km long, dated 3630–3375 BCE) predates Stonehenge itself by centuries, aligns with the equinox sunrise, and terminates near a long barrow. The **Dorset Cursus** (10 km, c. 3300 BCE) aligns with the midwinter sunset over a long barrow on Gussage Down. Functions debated — processional ways, boundaries between living and dead — but emphatically ceremonial, not domestic.

### 3.3 Long barrows and chambered tombs (c. 3800–3200 BCE)

**West Kennet** (Wiltshire, c. 3650 BCE): five stone-chambered compartments, ~46 disarticulated individuals reorganized over centuries before final blocking c. 2500 BCE. **Wayland's Smithy** (Oxfordshire, c. 3430 BCE): two-phase, with an earlier timber mortuary structure overbuilt by a stone cairn within ~50 years. **Fussell's Lodge** (Wiltshire, Ashbee 1957): purely earthen, ~57 disarticulated individuals with skulls and long bones systematically removed for circulation.

**Hazleton North** (Gloucestershire, Saville 1979–82) is the watershed site for Neolithic kinship: aDNA work by **Chris Fowler, Iñigo Olalde, Vicki Cummings, David Reich and colleagues** (_Nature_, 2021) reconstructed the **world's oldest family tree** from 27 of 35 buried individuals — five generations, strict patrilineal descent. Most descended from **four women who had children with one man** (evidence for polygyny in the founding generation). Maternal-line descent determined which chamber a person was placed in. This demolishes egalitarian and matriarchal readings of communal burial: these tombs were **family crypts** controlled by patrilineal descent groups.

**Regional traditions:** Cotswold-Severn group (~200 cairns, lateral chambers, false portal facades); **Court cairns** (~390 in Ireland, 100 in southwest Scotland) with open forecourts; **Clyde cairns** of western Scotland; **portal tombs/dolmens** (>150 in Ireland) — the skeletal form left when surrounding cairn material is removed.

### 3.4 Passage tombs

The Atlantic megalithic tradition, transmitted along a Atlantic-facing arc from Iberia and Brittany.

- **Brú na Bóinne** (Boyne Valley, County Meath, c. 3300–2900 BCE): **Newgrange** (200,000 tonnes of cairn material, 19 m passage, midwinter sunrise illumination through engineered roof-box, observed by Michael O'Kelly 21 December 1967, 17-minute illumination); **Knowth** (two passages aligned to equinox sunrise/sunset, ~390 carved stones — the largest concentration of megalithic art in Europe, excavated by George Eogan 1962–2000); **Dowth** (midwinter sunset passage). A 2017 aDNA study (Cassidy et al.) identified one Newgrange individual as the product of first-degree incest — suggesting a hereditary elite practicing dynastic endogamy of the kind documented later in Egypt and Hawaii.
- **Loughcrew, Carrowkeel, Carrowmore** (Ireland): mountaintop ridges, cremation, equinox and solstice alignments. Carrowmore is among the oldest megalithic cemeteries in Europe (c. 3800–2800 BCE).
- **Maeshowe** (Orkney, c. 2800 BCE): corbelled chamber, midwinter sunset alignment. The Barnhouse Stone, 760 m southwest, may serve as a horizon foresight (noted by Magnus Spence 1893). No human remains survived in situ — possibly removed by 12th-century Norse who left over 30 runic inscriptions.
- **Bryn Celli Ddu** (Anglesey, c. 3000 BCE): summer solstice sunrise alignment, originally a henge converted to a passage tomb.

### 3.5 The Avebury complex

The largest stone circle and henge in the world. **Avebury Henge** (outer circle originally ≥98 sarsens, 331.6 m circumference) was constructed in overlapping phases through the third millennium BCE. The ditch is **interior to the bank** — defining the bounded sacred interior, not defending against external enemies. **West Kennet Avenue** (2.4 km of paired stones to the Sanctuary) choreographed processional movement. **Silbury Hill** (c. 2400–2300 BCE, 39.3 m, 5 ha) is the largest prehistoric man-made mound in Europe, contains no burial, and has been variously interpreted as composite seasonal-ritual structure (Dames), territorial marker, ancestor monument, or axis mundi.

### 3.6 Stonehenge

Built in phases from c. 3000 to 1500 BCE.

- **Phase 1 (c. 3000 BCE):** Bank, ditch, two entrances, and the 56 **Aubrey Holes** containing cremated bone — at minimum 63, possibly 150–240 individuals over five centuries. Stonehenge began as the largest cremation cemetery of the third millennium BCE in the British Isles. Strontium isotope analysis (Snoeck, Schulting, Sheridan et al., _Scientific Reports_, 2018) showed that at least 10 of 25 cremated individuals had childhoods on igneous geology — most likely west Wales, the same region as the bluestones. **Pilgrimage was foundational.**
- **Phase 3I (c. 2600 BCE):** ~80 **bluestones** from Preseli quarries (Craig Rhos-y-felin, Carn Goedog) erected. Parker Pearson (_Antiquity_, 2021) proposed the bluestones were dismantled from an earlier circle at **Waun Mawn** in Pembrokeshire — Stonehenge as a translocated ancestral monument. Hypothesis is contested but generative.
- **Phase 3II (c. 2500 BCE):** **Sarsens** from **West Woods, Wiltshire** (~25 km), confirmed geochemically by **David Nash, Jake Ciborowski, Parker Pearson, Tim Darvill and colleagues** (_Science Advances_, 2020). Thirty uprights forming the outer circle, five central trilithons, mortise-and-tenon joinery. Axis aligned to midsummer sunrise / midwinter sunset.
- **Altar Stone (2024 finding):** **Anthony Clarke, Christopher Kirkland, Richard Bevins, Nick Pearce, Stijn Glorie, Rob Ixer** (_Nature_, August 2024) traced the Altar Stone not to Wales but to the **Orcadian Basin in northeast Scotland** — a 750 km transport. Parker Pearson and colleagues (2024, _Archaeology International_) connect its recumbent placement to the recumbent stone circle tradition of northeast Scotland. Stonehenge is now understood as a monument deliberately assembled from materials drawn across the entire island.
- **The Avenue** runs 1.7 miles to the River Avon, following a periglacial ridge already aligned with the solstice axis (Mike Allen et al., 2016) — the natural alignment may have been the original reason the site was selected.

### 3.7 Durrington Walls (c. 2600–2450 BCE)

3 km from Stonehenge, the largest henge enclosure in Britain (~17 ha), surrounding seven excavated Neolithic house floors and a possible total of ~1,000 dwellings — a temporary aggregation of perhaps 4,000 people. The **Southern Circle** of timber posts aligned to midwinter solstice sunrise. An avenue ran to the Avon, aligned to midwinter solstice sunset. In **2020**, **Vincent Gaffney and colleagues** announced a circuit of large pits >2 km across surrounding the henge; 2025 follow-up confirmed at least 16 are deliberate prehistoric features, implying a formal ceremonial boundary at unprecedented scale.

### 3.8 Orkney complex

UNESCO "Heart of Neolithic Orkney." **Skara Brae** (c. 3180–2500 BCE, stone-built village), **Stones of Stenness** (c. 3100–2700 BCE, henge with 12 stones, central hearth/altar deposits), **Ring of Brodgar** (c. 2600–2400 BCE, originally ≥60 stones from at least seven different geological sources — possibly representing different communities), and the pivotal **Ness of Brodgar** (c. 3500–2200 BCE, excavated by Nick Card 2003–2024). Ness produced ~36 structures including "Structure 10" — a ceremonial building with stone dressers and over 70 painted designs in ochre, the earliest evidence of painted buildings in Britain. Around 2400–2200 BCE the site was deliberately closed with a feasting event involving **~400 cattle tibias** cracked for marrow — one of the most dramatic documented ritual events in British prehistory.

### 3.9 Stone circles broadly

~1,000 henges and stone circles known. **Callanish (Calanais)** (Lewis, c. 2900–2600 BCE) — cruciform setting aligned to the major lunar standstill. **Castlerigg** (Cumbria, c. 3000 BCE) — 38 stones in mountain amphitheatre. **Long Meg and Her Daughters** (Cumbria, c. 3500–3000 BCE, ~109 m diameter, midwinter sunset, with cup-and-ring marks on the outlier stone). Wainwright's synthesis emphasized feasting, ritual, exchange, and social discourse as combined functions.

## 4. Burial, Excarnation, and the Treatment of the Dead

The dominant pattern is **disarticulation**: bones reach the tomb already separated and incomplete, indicating staged mortuary process.

**Excarnation evidence:** cut marks on bones (Aldestrop, West Tump, Wayland's Smithy); canid gnaw marks (controlled exposure to dogs/wolves); systematic underrepresentation of small bones (phalanges, carpals, tarsals — consistent with raised-platform exposure); overrepresentation of skulls and long bones in tomb deposits (selective recovery and curation).

**Bone circulation:** skulls and long bones moved between sites, used as relics, sometimes polished from repeated handling. Julian Thomas's "Rethinking the Neolithic" interpretation: the boundary between living persons and ancestral remains was deliberately permeable. The dead remained social actors. Mark Edmonds (_Ancestral Geographies of the Neolithic_, 1999) developed this socially: bone circulation enacted webs of kinship, obligation, and local authority across the landscape.

**Stonehenge cremation cemetery:** ~150–240 individuals cremated and buried in the Aubrey Holes between c. 3000–2500 BCE. The southeast quadrant cluster corresponds to the moon's most southerly rising position, suggesting a possible early lunar dimension to the site.

**Selectivity:** The MNI counts for long barrows (West Kennet ~46, Fussell's Lodge ~57, Hazleton North 35, Wayland's Smithy I 14, II 8) represent a small selection of all who died over the centuries each tomb operated. Monumental burial was a **right**, not a default. Hazleton North showed that right was patrilineally inherited.

**Cave use** — Elbolton, Fox Hole, Mendip caves — provided a third mortuary context, blurring the monument/natural-place distinction. Mendip caves yielded the largest assemblage of Grooved Ware outside henge contexts.

**Ritual violence:** Schulting and Wysocki's survey of ~350 Neolithic crania found a ~8.6% rate of trauma consistent with interpersonal violence — predominantly male, with projectile injuries almost exclusively male. The pattern suggests raiding rather than systematic sacrifice. Hambledon Hill yielded skull fragments and bones with embedded arrowheads.

## 5. Cosmology and Belief Systems

### 5.1 What we can support

- **Solar cult evidence is the strongest physical case.** Solstice alignments at Newgrange (winter sunrise), Maeshowe (winter sunset), Bryn Celli Ddu (summer sunrise), Stonehenge (solar axis bidirectional), Long Meg (winter sunset). The engineering investment is too precise to be incidental.
- **Lunar engagement is real but more contested.** Callanish at major lunar standstill (18.6-year cycle); recumbent stone circles of Aberdeenshire; Stonehenge Station Stones rectangle whose long sides align with lunar standstill extremes, only because of the site's specific latitude.
- **Ancestor veneration as primary religious focus** is the most robustly evidenced model. Bones were curated, circulated, redeposited; access to passage tombs was maintained for centuries; the social geography of the living was organized around the repositories of the dead. Renfrew (1976), Whittle, Bradley, Edmonds, Parker Pearson all converge here.
- **Stone-versus-wood binary** (Parker Pearson and Ramilisonina, 1998): wood for the living (Durrington Walls, Woodhenge, timber circles), stone for the ancestors and dead (Stonehenge). Drawn from Madagascar Merina ethnography as heuristic, not homology — but generative and increasingly well-supported by Stonehenge Riverside Project evidence.
- **Animism** in Vicki Cummings' careful reading: stones chosen for material qualities (translucent quartz, locally-prominent rock types) suggesting they were understood as having properties beyond the purely physical — without committing to any specific "animism" doctrine.

### 5.2 What the evidence does not support

- **Named deities.** None can be reconstructed; this requires linguistic record.
- **Polytheism in any structured sense.**
- **The Marija Gimbutas "Mother Goddess" thesis.** Hodder, Meskell and others have shown that the figurine corpus Gimbutas drew on does not unambiguously represent a unified female deity even in southeast Europe. Britain has almost no anthropomorphic figurines; the evidence base does not transfer. Chris Scarre has analyzed the British absence of figurines explicitly. The chalk so-called "goddess" from Grimes Graves is now widely doubted as authentic.
- **A formalized priesthood.** No unambiguous "priest graves" exist for the British or Irish Neolithic. Specialist knowledge clearly existed (cosmological, astronomical, organizational), but its institutional form is unknown.
- **Druidic religion.** As established in Section 0 — Iron Age, not Neolithic.

### 5.3 Major contested zones

- The **Folkton Drums** (three carved chalk cylinders, child burial, c. 2600–2000 BCE) and the **Burton Agnes Drum** (c. 3005–2890 BCE, child burial of three children) bear nearly identical motifs that recur on Grooved Ware, carved stone balls, and passage tomb art. They evidence a pan-British symbolic vocabulary. Whether they encode goddess imagery, cosmological schema, prestige language, calendar, or measuring tool remains genuinely unknown.
- **Bull/aurochs symbolism:** aurochs skulls in long barrow forecourts; "horned cairn" morphology; cattle skulls in hundreds at later Bronze Age sites with Neolithic antecedents. Likely symbolic, exact theology unrecoverable.
- **Three-realm cosmology** (sky/earth/underworld with standing stones as axis mundi) is a comparative-religion hypothesis projected onto the British evidence. Speculative, not derivable.

## 6. Astronomy and the Ritual Calendar

### 6.1 The methodological frame

Archaeoastronomy split decisively in the 1980s between Hawkins-Thom-style precision-observatory claims and the more rigorous statistical-and-contextual approach of **Clive Ruggles** (Leicester, _Astronomy in Prehistoric Britain and Ireland_, 1999; _Handbook of Archaeoastronomy_, Springer 2015). Ruggles' principle: alignments at British monuments are approximate (within 1–2 degrees), and sites were sacred landscapes with celestial dimensions — not functional observatories.

**Aubrey Burl's** complementary caution: stone circles were sites of ritual, fertility rites, ancestor worship, not precision instruments. **Ronald Hutton** (_The Pagan Religions of the Ancient British Isles_, 1991; _Stations of the Sun_, 1996) places these alignments within a broader argument about the limits of inference: orientation can be measured, theology cannot.

### 6.2 Hierarchy of evidence

**Well-attested (intentionality not seriously contested):**

- Newgrange winter solstice sunrise illumination via roof-box
- Stonehenge solstice axis (solar, bidirectional)
- Bryn Celli Ddu summer solstice sunrise
- Durrington Walls midwinter feasting (zooarchaeological — pig tooth eruption seasonality, Wright et al. 2014)

**Probable (strong evidence, contested details):**

- Maeshowe winter solstice sunset (alignment real; precise day of solstice illumination disputed by Ruggles given mean axis at 221°)
- Stonehenge Station Stones / major lunar standstill (Newham 1966; under investigation by Ruggles, Chadburn, Silva at the 2024–2025 standstill; cremation southeast cluster may be early lunar evidence)
- Callanish major lunar standstill (Curtis, Ponting; statistical orientation evidence)
- Recumbent Stone Circles of Aberdeenshire (Ruggles and Burl 1985: trend toward southern lunar arc through the recumbent)

**Speculative or rejected:**

- **Tim Darvill's 2022 sarsen calendar hypothesis** (365.25-day perpetual calendar inscribed in the stones, Egyptian analogue): rejected by Magli and Belmonte (_Antiquity_, 2023) on grounds that the number 12 appears nowhere structurally, leap-year mechanism is missing, sun moves too slowly near solstices for the claimed precision, and the Egyptian civil calendar postdates Stonehenge by ~2,000 years.
- **Alexander Thom's megalithic yard** (0.829 m precision standard): falsified by Heggie's statistical reanalysis; Burl called it "a chimera, a grotesque statistical misconception."
- **Hawkins' eclipse predictor:** refuted by Atkinson and Ruggles.
- **British Neolithic Pleiades or Orion alignments:** no rigorous evidence; pattern-matching of the type Ruggles' methodology explicitly rejects.

### 6.3 The lunar-to-solar question

The cremation evidence at Stonehenge clusters in the southeast — near the southernmost lunar standstill rising direction — during the earliest phase. The later sarsen phase emphasizes the solar axis. There is meaningful but not conclusive evidence that the monument may have been **reorganized from a primarily lunar to a primarily solar emphasis** between c. 3000 and 2500 BCE. This remains debated.

### 6.4 Mesolithic continuity

Beneath the modern Stonehenge car park, four large pine post-holes dated c. 8000 BCE — 5,000 years before Stonehenge — three roughly aligned east-west. Sample size too small for statistical claim of intentionality, but they show the location had human significance millennia before the henge. Contemporaneous Mesolithic activity is documented at Blick Mead nearby. Whether anything cosmological survived the Mesolithic-Neolithic demographic break is unresolved.

## 7. Material Culture and Ritual Artifacts

### 7.1 Polished stone axes

Three production centers structure the evidence:

- **Great Langdale** (Cumbria, Group VI): ~1,661 axeheads over 250–750 years (2–7/year). Quarry faces sit on dangerously steep ledges near Pike o' Stickle's summit — inaccessibility deliberate, possibly to bring the act of extraction "close to the sky." Many recovered from rivers, bogs, and monuments without use-wear. Mark Edmonds: "the life of the axe" as continuous ritual biography.
- **Graig Lwyd** (Conwy, Group VII): ~387 axeheads over ~500 years. Fragments brought to ceremonial sites and ritually disaggregated in pits.
- **Alpine jadeitite** (Monte Viso, Monte Beigua): ~1,800 across Europe, ~30 reaching Scotland. **Projet JADE** (Pétrequin, Sheridan): some traveled up to 1,700 km, growing in prestige with each exchange. Many never hafted; some polished to mirror finish in Britain. The **Mortlake jadeite axe** (Thames, c. 4000 BCE, unused) is hard to explain as casual loss.

Use-wear analysis (_Journal of Archaeological Science_, 2023) confirms some Langdale axes were used practically — but wet and monument deposits remain non-utilitarian. Functions are not mutually exclusive within an axe's life history.

### 7.2 Pottery sequence

- **Carinated Bowl** (c. 4000–3600 BCE): thin-walled, burnished, continental LBK/Michelsberg derivation.
- **Peterborough Wares** (Ebbsfleet, Mortlake, Fengate, c. 3800–3000 BCE): heavily impressed decoration, found in causewayed enclosures and pits.
- **Grooved Ware** (c. 3200–2400 BCE): originated in Orkney (Ness of Brodgar, Skara Brae), spread south to Wessex by c. 2800 BCE. Flat-bottomed, geometric grooved decoration. Four sub-styles: Rinyo, Woodlands, Clacton, Durrington Walls. **Uniquely associated with henge monuments and feasting.** Residue analysis at Durrington shows animal fats; some vessels apparently held black henbane (_Hyoscyamus niger_).
- **Beaker** (from c. 2400 BCE): inverted-bell shape, geometric incised decoration; arrives with the Beaker package and the demographic replacement.

### 7.3 Carved chalk and stone objects

- **Folkton Drums** (Yorkshire, c. 2600–2000 BCE, three drums, child burial; British Museum)
- **Burton Agnes Drum** (East Yorkshire, c. 3005–2890 BCE, child burial of three; revealed 2022; described by British Museum curator Neil Wilkin as "the most important piece of prehistoric art to be found in Britain in the last 100 years")
- **Carved stone balls** of Scotland (~425–525 known, mostly Aberdeenshire, c. 3200–2500 BCE, ~70 mm diameter, 3–160 protruding knobs; uniformity suggests standardization; the **Towie Ball** has motifs paralleling Newgrange). No functional consensus; possibilities include weapons, weights, mnemonic devices, ritual objects.
- **Maceheads:** the **Knowth Macehead** (3300–2800 BCE, pale flint with apparent anthropomorphic face); the **Maesmor Macehead** (Wales, ~3200–2800 BCE, near-identical to Knowth — Irish Sea cultural connection). Fiona Roe's 1968 typology (ovoid, Maesmor, flint nodule, Thames pestle, Orkney pestle) remains foundational.

### 7.4 Flint, mining, and "fancy lithics"

- **Grimes Graves** (Norfolk, c. 2650 BCE, >430 mine shafts): high-quality "floorstone" flint, deliberately structured deposits in galleries (Pit 15: Cornish greenstone axe + antler picks tine-inward + phalarope skull). Mining itself ritually charged.
- **Cissbury** (Sussex): red ochre on gallery walls and carved anthropomorphic motifs.
- **Flint daggers** (c. 2400–2000 BCE): pressure-flaked, leaf-shaped, prestige display objects of the Beaker transition.

### 7.5 Animal bone deposits and feasting

**Durrington Walls** is the paradigm: tens of thousands of pig and cattle bones, multi-isotope analysis by **Richard Madgwick et al.** (_Science Advances_, 2019) showing pigs from west Wales, upland northeast England, and northeast Scotland — distances of 100 to >600 km. Animals were transported as food. Feasting participants brought their own animals — a prescribed ritual obligation. Cattle isotope studies (2019) showed ~80% non-local origins.

Parasitological evidence (2023) — fish tapeworm (_Diphyllobothrium_) and liver fluke from Durrington Walls — confirms participants from inland and upland communities, not just chalk-downland locals.

### 7.6 Color and personal ornament

White chalk and quartz (passage tomb facades, Newgrange's reconstructed quartz frontage); **red ochre** (on bones, gallery walls, rock art); jet from Whitby, Kimmeridge shale, amber from the Baltic — long-distance prestige networks for personal display. Wear analysis shows some amber beads were heirlooms at deposition; others made specifically for burial.

## 8. Water, Bog, and River Ritual

The Neolithic religious landscape was made not only of fields and tombs but of **watery places** — rivers, wetlands, floodplains, bog margins, marsh crossings.

- **The Thames:** Roy Adkins and Ralph Jackson's corpus shows a Neolithic axe-deposition tradition; the Mortlake jadeite axe is exemplary. Finds from Hammersmith, Twickenham, Teddington, Greenwich. **Belmarsh West** trackways (Hart et al., _Proceedings of the Prehistoric Society_, 2015) date to the first quarter of the 4th millennium cal BCE — the wetland was already being formalized as socially important.
- **The Trent:** Three Group VI Cumbrian stone axes found together at Netherfield, Nottinghamshire — a grouped river deposit of imported axes consistent with selective deposition.
- **Etton causewayed enclosure** (Cambridgeshire, Pryor; reanalysis Beadsmoore, Garrow, Knight): floodplain island, intermittent and structured deposition. Best Neolithic precursor to later wetland traditions.
- **The Somerset Levels:** Coles and Hibbert documented the **Sweet Track**; recent work at Honeygar pushes some routeway construction to 3770–3640 BCE. Repeated maintenance and formal placement imply socially charged crossings.
- **Outer Hebrides crannogs:** Garrow and Sturt (_Antiquity_, 2019) documented Neolithic crannogs with deposition of pottery into surrounding water between c. 3720 and 2600 cal BCE.

Richard Bradley (_The Passage of Arms_; _The Archaeology of Natural Places_) provides the synthesis: wet places were liminal zones between the living world and an otherworld; objects deposited as offerings or transformations. Comparable Danish "bog pots" (Robson et al.) and other northern European parallels reinforce the interpretation. Britain fits an Atlantic-European **watery-places tradition** where water was an active medium of transaction.

## 9. Rock Art and the Symbolic Vocabulary

### 9.1 The two corpora

- **Open-air cup-and-ring marks:** Northumberland (Roughting Linn, Doddington, Old Bewick), Galloway (Drumtroddan), Kilmartin Glen (Achnabreck — the largest single rock-art panel in Scotland — Cairnbaan, Kilmichael Glassary, Baluachraig). Documented systematically by **Stan Beckensall**; aggregated in the **England's Rock Art** project. Dates imprecise (c. 3500–1200 BCE).
- **Megalithic art:** primarily in Ireland (Knowth, Newgrange, Dowth, Loughcrew); in Wales (Barclodiad y Gawres, Bryn Celli Ddu); in Orkney (Maeshowe surroundings, Holm of Papa Westray). The Eogan and Twohig catalog of Knowth alone includes ~390 carved stones, accounting for ~46% of Ireland's megalithic art.

### 9.2 Motif vocabulary

Open-air: cupmarks, cup-and-ring marks, concentric circles, penannular motifs, grooves, gutters.
Megalithic: spirals (single, double, triple), lozenges, chevrons/zigzags, concentric circles, radials, serpentiniforms, "eye" motifs, triangles. The **Newgrange entrance stone (Kerbstone 1)** with its triple spirals and lozenges, and **Kerbstone 52** at the rear, are iconic.

### 9.3 Interpretation

- **Richard Bradley** (1989 onward): contextual reading — meaning emerges through movement, topography, and monument use. Comparison across Britain, Ireland, Brittany, Iberia.
- **Clive Waddington:** phased sequence in northern Britain, beginning with symbolic marking of natural rock, later incorporated into monumental settings.
- **Andrew Meirion Jones:** meaning lies in the **process of making and reworking** as much as in motif.
- **Guillaume Robin** (Irish passage tombs, 2010): "spatial syntax" of signs within architecture — meaning depends on placement at thresholds, in passages, in chambers.

### 9.4 Comparative Atlantic context

Irish Sea passage tomb art is overwhelmingly **geometric**; Breton and Iberian funerary art more often **figurative**. Recent work on **Barnenez** in Brittany suggests painted motifs and Atlantic links, but underscores regional variation. The honest synthesis: Neolithic communities around the Atlantic façade shared certain habits of **abstract monumental marking** without sharing one symbolic language.

Mainstream archaeology now rejects "star maps," "fertility codes," and "hidden scripts." The defensible model: rock art and megalithic carving as **place-making** — marking movement routes, thresholds, tomb interiors, and rocks already charged with significance.

## 10. Shamanism, Altered States, and Psychoactive Plants

The most contested angle. Direct evidence for shamans in Neolithic Britain and Ireland is weak.

### 10.1 The Lewis-Williams entoptic model

**J. David Lewis-Williams and Thomas Dowson** (_Current Anthropology_, 1988) argued recurring geometric forms in prehistoric art derive from **entoptic phenomena** seen in altered states. **Jeremy Dronfield** applied this most explicitly to Knowth and Newgrange, proposing spirals, lozenges, zigzags, and the tunnel-like passage experience as visionary-state material.

There is real basis for taking the question seriously: passage tomb art is repetitive, structured, and tied to restricted architectural movement, darkness, controlled illumination, and bodily experience. **But the leap from altered states to "shamanism" is not secure.** Chris Fowler has summarized the objections: geometric motifs need not be entoptic; altered states need not require shamans; "shamanism" is too broad to explain a sedentary farming society. This caution is now mainstream.

### 10.2 Psychoactive plants

- **Henbane:** the famous claim of residues on Grooved Ware from **Balfarg/Balbirnie** (Fife) was overturned by **Deborah Long et al.** (_Journal of Archaeological Science_, 1999) — no evidence for henbane or other poisonous plants.
- **Fly agaric:** no good archaeological case for British Neolithic use.
- **Opium poppy:** **Aurélie Salavert** and colleagues confirm secure Neolithic _Papaver somniferum_ presence and dispersal in western Europe, but evidence demonstrates cultivation and movement, not ritual intoxication, and not directly for Britain.

### 10.3 Mesolithic precedent

**Star Carr** (North Yorkshire, c. 9000 BCE): 24 modified red deer antler frontlets — interpreted by **Chantal Conneller** as bodily transformation, by **Aimee Little** and colleagues (2016, _PLOS ONE_) as possible shamanic costumes — the oldest known evidence of probable shamanic practice in Europe. But Star Carr lies thousands of years before Stonehenge, the populations are genetically different, and continuity of practice across the demographic break is undemonstrable.

The responsible conclusion: Neolithic Britain and the Atlantic Neolithic show evidence for powerful ritual settings, restricted access, symbolic art, and possibly altered-state practices. **"Shamanic" as a system label remains hypothesis, not settled fact.**

## 11. Society: Specialists, Pilgrimage, Kinship, Communal Labor

### 11.1 Was there a priesthood?

No unambiguous "priest graves." Causewayed enclosures, structured deposition, and monument coordination imply **specialist knowledge** existed — cosmological, astronomical, organizational — but not necessarily an institutionalized hereditary priesthood. Sheridan and Whittle both note that monumentality required organizational expertise without that expertise needing to be formalized.

### 11.2 Gender

Long barrows like Hazleton North show patrilineal structuring with selective female inclusion. Strontium and oxygen isotope studies (Neil 2017 and others) suggest later Neolithic women may have been **more mobile than men** — patrilocal residence with women moving at marriage. The near-absence of female figurines in British Neolithic contexts argues against an explicitly feminine divine focus, in contrast to southeast European Neolithic traditions.

### 11.3 The Amesbury Archer

Excavated 2002 near Stonehenge, c. 2340–2300 BCE. Beaker-period transitional figure: five Beaker pots, 16 flint arrowheads, three copper knives, two gold hair ornaments (the oldest gold from Britain), cushion stones (metalworking), boar tusks. Strontium and oxygen isotopes show he grew up in the Alpine region of central Europe — an immigrant arriving as Stonehenge's sarsens were being erected. His "Companion" had similar isotopes, possibly a relative. Specialist roles: coppersmith, archer, fire-keeper. Whether these were "religious" is speculative; metalworking in the Early Bronze Age was associated with transformative knowledge.

### 11.4 Pilgrimage at scale

The Durrington Walls isotope evidence (Section 7.5) is decisive: people and animals from across Britain converged for midwinter feasts. This is the first clear evidence of **Britain-wide ceremonial connectivity** — pilgrimage as a religious institution.

### 11.5 Communal labor as religious act

Sarsen transport (~25 km, 10–30 tonne stones, no wheels or draft animals); bluestone transport (~250 km from Preseli); the Waun Mawn translocation hypothesis (Stonehenge as transplanted ancestral monument); Newgrange's 200,000 tonnes of cairn material; Silbury Hill's 5 hectares. Cumulative labor for the major monument complexes is in millions of person-hours. There was no state to coerce this work — it was motivated participation. Powerful collective ideological commitment is the only viable explanation.

### 11.6 Kinship — Hazleton and beyond

The **Hazleton North** five-generation pedigree (Fowler, Olalde et al., _Nature_, 2021) showed strict patrilineal descent, evidence for polygyny, and maternal-line determination of chamber placement. **In April 2026**, Cummings, Fowler, Olalde, Reich and colleagues extended this picture across Caithness and Orkney tombs, describing **"webs of descent"** — tombs as active technologies for materializing ancestry, descent, and belonging.

For Irish passage tombs, work emerging from UCD and the Reich collaborations suggests broader genetic relatedness — an apparent **dynastic elite network**: the Listoghil individual at Carrowmore shares detectable kinship with individuals at Newgrange, Millin Bay, and Carrowkeel. Stable isotopes show this elite ate more animal protein than the general Neolithic population.

## 12. Continental and Atlantic Connections

Neolithic religion in Britain was not self-generated. Farming arrived c. 4000 BCE carried by migrants whose continental ancestry traces through Iberian Neolithic populations and ultimately Aegean farmers. With them came:

- **The TRB (Funnel Beaker / Trichterbecherkultur)** of northern Europe, primary builders of megalithic passage graves across Denmark, North Germany, southern Scandinavia. Communal burial, mixing of ancestral bones, passage orientation, quartz use — all attested on both sides of the North Sea.
- **The Michelsberg culture** (Rhine/Seine basin, c. 4400–3500 BCE), builders of causewayed enclosures parallel to British examples.
- **The Atlantic megalithic tradition:** Barnenez (Finistère, c. 4500–4200 BCE) — earlier than any British monument; **Gavrinis** (Morbihan, c. 4200–4000 BCE) with elaborate carved orthostat decoration paralleling Newgrange and Knowth; the **Carnac alignments** (Morbihan, c. 4500–2500 BCE).
- **The Bell Beaker network** (from c. 2400 BCE) bringing renewed continental contact, individual burial, copper, archery, and fundamentally new ideological frameworks that displaced or transformed the passage-grave ancestor cult.

## 13. The Beaker Transition — Religious Disruption, c. 2400 BCE

This is the most decisive religious discontinuity in British prehistory before Christianity. The shifts were not stylistic but cosmological:

| Neolithic                             | Beaker                                    |
| ------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| Communal long barrows / chamber tombs | Individual round-barrow inhumation        |
| Multi-generational, lineage-focused   | Personal, biographical                    |
| No grave goods (mostly)               | Beaker pot + dagger + wristguard + arrows |
| Grooved Ware feasting at henges       | Personal drinking vessel                  |
| Bones of the dead as ongoing presence | Person buried whole and intact            |
| Lineage as unit of commemoration      | Individual as unit                        |

The Olalde 2018 aDNA data show ~90% population replacement within centuries. Whether the Beaker arrival involved peaceful migration, violent displacement, or differential fertility is debated. Lack of admixture or transition period in early Beaker British individuals suggests rapid replacement; some scholars (Haak) read this as suggesting conflict, others as competitive reproductive replacement over generations. The Amesbury Archer exemplifies the new order — personal agency, specialist knowledge, individual status — a completely different religious grammar from Hazleton North fifty generations earlier.

## 14. Recent Discoveries (2020–2026)

The 2020s have transformed the field by clarifying **scale, connectivity, and kinship** rather than revealing named deities.

- **2020 — Sarsen sourcing:** Nash, Ciborowski, Parker Pearson, Darvill et al. (_Science Advances_) confirmed West Woods, Wiltshire (~25 km) as the source of most Stonehenge sarsens via geochemical analysis of Stone 58.
- **2020 — Durrington pits ring:** Vincent Gaffney and colleagues identified a circuit of large pits >2 km across surrounding Durrington Walls.
- **2021 — Hazleton North family tree:** Fowler, Olalde, Cummings, Reich et al. (_Nature_) reconstructed a five-generation patrilineal pedigree, reshaping Neolithic kinship understanding.
- **2022 — Burton Agnes Drum:** Neil Wilkin, Mark Allen, Alice Beasley revealed the c. 3005–2890 BCE chalk drum from a child triple burial — a Grooved Ware-period symbolic peer of the Folkton Drums and a key to the pan-British symbolic vocabulary.
- **2022 — Darvill solar calendar hypothesis:** Tim Darvill (_Antiquity_) proposed a 365.25-day calendar in the sarsens — rejected by Magli and Belmonte (2023).
- **2023 — Durrington parasitology:** Fish tapeworm and liver fluke evidence confirmed pan-British origins of feasters.
- **2024 — Altar Stone Scottish origin:** Anthony Clarke, Chris Kirkland, Richard Bevins, Nick Pearce, Stijn Glorie, Rob Ixer (_Nature_, August 2024) traced the Altar Stone to the Orcadian Basin in northeast Scotland — a 750 km transport. Parker Pearson et al. (2024) link this to the recumbent stone circle tradition of northeast Scotland.
- **2024–2025 — Major lunar standstill at Stonehenge:** English Heritage, Oxford, Leicester (Ruggles), Bournemouth (Silva, Chadburn) collaboration documenting lunar alignments at the Station Stones during the 18.6-year cycle. Ruggles: "there are some bits of evidence that made me think that it was deliberate."
- **2025 — Cuckoo Stone and Tor Stone:** Phil Harding, David Nash, Jake Ciborowski et al. argued these were also moved from West Woods and may have formed an earlier "portal" into the Stonehenge landscape.
- **2025 — Newgrange winter feasting:** Eric Guiry, Fiona Beglane, Neil Carlin and colleagues (_Proceedings of the Prehistoric Society_) showed feasting c. 2600–2450 BCE focused on woodland-pannaged pigs, probably tied to the winter solstice.
- **2025 — Durrington pits confirmation:** Gaffney et al. follow-up confirmed at least 16 pits as deliberate prehistoric features.
- **2025–2026 — Glacial-transport hypothesis weakened:** Bevins (2025) and Clarke (January 2026) work against glacial explanations for Stonehenge's exotic stones; intentional human transport is now favored.
- **2026 — "Webs of descent" in Caithness/Orkney:** Cummings, Fowler, Olalde, Reich et al. (_Antiquity_) extended the kinship-through-tomb model across northern Scotland.

## 15. What We Know vs. What We Don't

**What the evidence supports:**

- Religion organized around the agricultural cycle, with midwinter as the major ritual moment.
- Solstice and (probably) lunar standstill alignments built into monumental architecture.
- Ancestor veneration as the most archaeologically supported religious focus.
- Wood/stone binary mapped to living/dead.
- Pan-British pilgrimage networks centered on Stonehenge–Durrington complex by c. 2500 BCE.
- A pan-British/Irish symbolic vocabulary (lozenges, spirals, concentric circles, chevrons) appearing across pottery, chalk drums, carved stone balls, and tomb art.
- Patrilineal kinship structuring access to communal burial, with polygyny attested.
- Selective deposition of valued objects in rivers, bogs, and wetlands as a major religious idiom.
- Atlantic-facing continental cultural transmission via Iberia, Brittany, and northern European TRB.
- A demographic and ideological rupture c. 2400 BCE with the Bell Beaker arrival.

**What the evidence does NOT support:**

- Named deities of any kind.
- The Druids.
- The Marija Gimbutas "Mother Goddess" thesis as applied to Britain.
- A formalized, hereditary priesthood with archaeologically distinct burials.
- Alexander Thom's megalithic yard or precision astronomical observatories.
- Tim Darvill's 2022 sarsen-calendar hypothesis.
- Stonehenge as eclipse predictor.
- Pleiades or Orion stellar alignments.
- Systematic ritual sacrifice (interpersonal violence is attested at low rates; sacrifice is not).
- Continuous shamanic tradition from Mesolithic into Neolithic.

**What remains genuinely contested:**

- Lunar-alignment intentionality at Stonehenge Station Stones (active research at the 2024–2025 standstill).
- Specific meanings of Folkton/Burton Agnes drum motifs.
- Whether passage tomb art encodes altered states (entoptic model).
- The Waun Mawn–Stonehenge translocation hypothesis.
- The relative weight of summer versus winter solstice in the Stonehenge ritual cycle.
- The mechanism of the Beaker replacement (peaceful, violent, demographic).
- Whether any element of Neolithic religion survived through the Beaker transition.

## 16. The People Themselves

The communities who built these monuments were genetically the descendants of Anatolian/Aegean farmers who reached Britain via Iberia and the Atlantic. They lived in small-scale societies organized around patrilineal kin groups; they kept cattle, pigs, and sheep, dairied widely, grew emmer wheat and barley, and managed woodland. They aggregated periodically — sometimes from across the entire island — for ceremonies that involved feasting, processing the dead, observing the sun and probably the moon, exchanging objects of charged biographical history, and depositing valuables in water and in the ground. They built monuments at extraordinary scales, knew themselves to be descended from those who came before them, and treated bones as ongoing social actors. Their religion has no name. It left no creed. What it left was a landscape that still works: Newgrange still admits the midwinter sun; Stonehenge's axis still tracks the solstices; cup-and-ring marks at Kilmartin still mark the rocks they were carved into 5,000 years ago. The communities themselves were largely replaced within centuries of finishing Stonehenge's sarsens. What we read in the monuments is, in a real sense, the religion of a people who no longer exist and whose descendants form a small fraction of modern British ancestry.
