# Neolithic Religion in Britain and Surrounding Areas, c. 4000-2200 BCE

## Introduction

Religion in Neolithic Britain has to be reconstructed from practices rather than texts. There are no scriptures, priestly lists, or named gods. What survives are monuments, deposits, food remains, carved symbols, and altered landscapes. For that reason, the safest archaeological approach is to speak of **ritual systems**, **sacred landscapes**, and **cosmological practice**, not fixed theology. Across Britain, Ireland, and the nearby Atlantic world, the period from the arrival of farming around **4000 BCE** to the later construction phases of **Stonehenge** saw a major shift: religion became increasingly tied to domesticated land, managed animals, monumental burial, watery places, and highly formalized seasonal gatherings.

The best-supported modern synthesis is cautious. Archaeologists such as **Richard Bradley**, **Mike Parker Pearson**, **Chris Fowler**, **Andrew Meirion Jones**, and **Vicki Cummings** generally reject single-key explanations such as “solar cult,” “fertility cult,” or “shaman religion.” Instead, the evidence points to overlapping systems: ancestral tomb cults, feasting and aggregation, selective deposition in rivers and wetlands, symbolic carving, and landscapes organized through paths, boundaries, and monument clusters. Stonehenge belongs inside that broader religious world, not outside it.

## 1. Environmental and Economic Religion

The arrival of farming in Britain after about **4000 BCE** did not simply change diet. It changed the religious meaning of land. Pollen, archaeobotany, and settlement evidence show woodland clearance, field-making, cereal cultivation, and livestock management, but not as one uniform wave. **Michael O'Connell** and **Karen Molloy** argued from Irish pollen records that the strongest environmental impact often belongs to the earlier Neolithic, with later episodes of woodland regeneration. That matters because it suggests that cleared land, regrown woodland, and managed edge zones all had social significance, rather than a simple march from forest to farmland.

Sites such as **Céide Fields** in County Mayo, traditionally dated to around **3500 BCE**, show a built agricultural landscape of walls, houses, and tombs. Whatever one makes of chronology debates raised by **Andrew Whitefield**, the key point is clear: “first fields” were not merely practical. They were territorial and probably sacred. Marking land with walls, enclosures, and tombs turned cultivation into a moral and ancestral claim on the landscape.

Archaeobotanical work by **Meriel McClatchie**, **Amy Bogaard**, and colleagues shows that cereals were widespread in Neolithic Ireland, especially in the earlier Neolithic, with **emmer wheat** dominant and **barley** also present. Comparable work in Wales by **Edward Treasure**, **Darren Grocke**, **Astrid Caseldine**, and **Mike Church** shows that cereals were culturally important even where archaeobotanical quantities remain modest. In other words, wheat and barley were not fringe foods. Their arrival marked a new ritual relationship with sowing, harvesting, storage, and seasonality.

The same is true for domestic animals. Organic residue studies by **Richard Evershed** and colleagues demonstrated widespread **dairying** in British Neolithic pottery. Cattle, sheep, and pigs were therefore more than meat reserves. Cattle and sheep provided renewable products, especially milk; pigs were central to periodic slaughter and large-scale feasting. These species carried different social meanings because they occupied different positions in the farming year.

Direct evidence for “sacred groves” in Neolithic Britain is weak, and it is methodologically better to say that **woodland retained ritual importance** than to claim proven grove cults. But woodland did remain deeply embedded in ritual life. Timber monuments are the clearest example. At **Woodhenge**, built around **2500 BCE**, cut timber was arranged in formal concentric settings aligned on the **summer solstice sunrise**. At nearby **Durrington Walls**, timber architecture was aligned to the **midwinter solstice sunrise**. The symbolic point is not just astronomy. Trees from managed woodland were transformed into ceremonial architecture that structured sacred time.

Late Neolithic food evidence makes the seasonal dimension even clearer. **Elizabeth Wright**, **Sarah Viner-Daniels**, **Mike Parker Pearson**, and **Umberto Albarella** showed that pigs at Durrington Walls were slaughtered mainly in **winter**, consistent with large periodic gatherings. **Richard Madgwick** and colleagues then demonstrated that some pigs consumed at Durrington and related Wessex ceremonial sites came from across Britain. In effect, livestock were being mobilized into ritual networks. The most dramatic recent parallel comes from Ireland: **Eric Guiry**, **Fiona Beglane**, **Neil Carlin**, and colleagues argued in 2025 that feasting at **Newgrange** around **2600-2450 BCE** focused on pigs fattened by woodland **pannage** and probably tied to the **winter solstice**. Here agriculture, managed woodland, feast animals, and sacred calendar all converge.

The safest conclusion is that Neolithic religion in Britain increasingly sacralized the **domesticated cycle**: clearing, planting, herding, milking, slaughtering, and gathering at key moments of the year. Wild resources did not disappear, but the religious landscape was now organized through the annual logic of farming.

## 2. Water, Bog, and River Ritual

The Neolithic religious landscape was not only made of fields and tombs. It was also made of **watery places**: rivers, wetlands, floodplains, bog margins, and routeways across marshes. Britain’s best-known river deposition traditions become much more spectacular in the Bronze and Iron Ages, but their roots begin earlier.

The **Thames** is central. At **Belmarsh West** in Greenwich, **Diccon Hart** and colleagues identified two superimposed **Early Neolithic trackways** in the Thames floodplain, radiocarbon dated to the first quarter of the **4th millennium cal BCE**. These were practical structures, but they also show that wetlands were already being formalized as socially important routes rather than ignored as marginal ground. In the same river system, the corpus assembled by **Roy Adkins** and **Ralph Jackson** shows a real Neolithic Thames tradition of axe finds. The **Mortlake jadeite axe**, an imported Alpine prestige object dated around **4000 BCE** and reportedly unused, is especially difficult to explain as casual loss. British Museum finds from places such as **Hammersmith**, **Twickenham**, **Teddington**, and **Greenwich** reinforce the pattern. Not every object in a river is votive, but the repeated presence of polished and sometimes unworn axes makes selective deposition plausible.

The **Trent** has thinner published evidence, yet even here the signal exists. At **Netherfield** in Nottinghamshire, the county Historic Environment Record notes three **Group VI stone axeheads** found together in the River Trent, with additional polished axes from the same area. A grouped river find of imported Cumbrian axes does not prove ritual, but it sits comfortably within a wider pattern of special deposition in water.

Wetland ceremonialism appears even more clearly in eastern England. At **Etton**, Cambridgeshire, **Francis Pryor** placed a causewayed enclosure on a floodplain island in a former meander of the Welland. Later reanalysis by **Emma Beadsmoore**, **Duncan Garrow**, and **Mark Knight** argued that deposition there was **intermittent, focused, and structured**, rather than mere rubbish disposal. That makes Etton one of the best Neolithic precursors to the later watery-place traditions of the Fens. The same broader basin later contained **Flag Fen**, and although Flag Fen’s most famous ritual deposits are Bronze Age, Pryor’s basin work and later syntheses show that the wetland crossing tradition itself began in the Neolithic.

The Somerset Levels provide a second major tradition. **John Coles** and **F. Alan Hibbert** documented Neolithic timber routeways such as the **Sweet Track**, and recent work at **Honeygar** has pushed some of this wetland route-building into the period **3770-3640 BCE**. Such structures were plainly useful, but their repeated maintenance and formal placement imply that crossing marsh and bog was socially charged. Wetlands were thresholds, not empty space.

Evidence for springs and wells is more ambiguous. The classic case of **Eaton Heath** near Norwich was once published as “ritual shafts and wells,” but later summaries tend to prefer an explanation as primarily wells. That is a good reminder that not every unusual water feature was cultic. Still, across Britain and nearby regions the repeated investment in routes, deposits, and monuments at wet margins supports the broader archaeological category of **watery places**.

Nearby Atlantic and north-west European comparanda make the interpretation stronger. **Duncan Garrow** and **Fraser Sturt** argued that Neolithic crannogs in the **Outer Hebrides** involved deposition of pottery into surrounding water between roughly **3720 and 2600 cal BCE**. In Denmark, **Harry Robson** and colleagues showed intentional deposition of Neolithic “bog pots” in bogs, rivers, and lakes. Britain fits that wider tradition. The religious implication is modest but important: water was treated as an active medium of transaction, passage, concealment, and offering.

## 3. Rock Art and Carved Symbols

British and Irish Neolithic religion also expressed itself through abstract carving. In open-air contexts the dominant vocabulary is **cupmarks, cup-and-ring marks, concentric circles, penannular motifs, grooves, and gutters**. In passage tombs the vocabulary expands to **spirals, lozenges, zigzags or chevrons, triangles, radials, and circles**.

The great concentrations are well known. In **Northumberland**, the core archive is now the **England's Rock Art** project, built in part from the work of **Stan Beckensall**, whose documentary legacy remains foundational. In south-west Scotland, **Drumtroddan** represents the Galloway tradition. In **Kilmartin Glen**, panels at **Achnabreck**, **Cairnbaan**, and **Kilmichael Glassary** include some of the most elaborate carvings in Britain. Historic Environment Scotland explicitly notes that the spirals at Achnabreck resemble Irish examples, suggesting Atlantic connections.

In Ireland, the major center is **Brú na Bóinne**. UNESCO describes **Knowth, Newgrange, and Dowth** as Europe’s largest concentration of megalithic art. The latest Knowth excavation volume by **George Eogan** and **Elizabeth Shee Twohig** catalogues roughly **390 carved stones** and suggests that Knowth alone contains about **46% of Ireland’s megalithic art**. At **Newgrange**, built around **3200 BCE**, the famous entrance stone and chamber triple spiral have understandably dominated public imagination, but from an archaeological perspective the important point is repetition: the motifs recur across structural stones and were integrated into tomb architecture. **Loughcrew** shows that open-air carvings and passage tomb art existed side by side, though with different levels of complexity.

Interpretation has changed significantly. **Richard Bradley** argued in 1989 and after that megalithic and rock art should be read contextually, through movement, topography, and monument use, not treated as an isolated catalogue of signs. His later work pushed comparison across Britain, Ireland, Brittany, and Iberia. **Clive Waddington** proposed a phased sequence in northern Britain, beginning with symbolic marking of natural rock and later incorporation into more monumental settings. **Andrew Meirion Jones** has argued that meaning lies not just in motif but in the process of making and reworking. For Irish passage tombs, **Guillaume Robin** emphasized the “spatial syntax” of signs within architecture: carvings mean something partly because of where they are placed in passages, thresholds, and chambers.

This is also where comparison with **Brittany** and **Iberia** matters. Atlantic archaeologists have long recognized connections among these regions, but not simple identity. Irish Sea passage-tomb art is overwhelmingly **geometric**, whereas Breton and Iberian funerary art is more often **figurative**. Recent work on **Barnenez** in Brittany suggests painted motifs and Atlantic links, but also underscores regional variation. The safest conclusion is not that all Atlantic megaliths encoded one symbolic language, but that Neolithic communities around the Atlantic façade shared certain habits of abstract monumental marking.

Mainstream archaeology now rejects simplistic explanations such as “star maps,” universal fertility codes, or hidden scripts. A more defensible synthesis is that rock art and megalithic carving were a form of **place-making**: they marked movement routes, thresholds, tomb interiors, and rocks already charged with significance. In that sense they were religious, but not because anyone has decoded a doctrine. They materialized repeated engagement with sacred places.

## 4. Shamanism, Altered States, and Psychoactive Plants

This is the most contested angle. Direct evidence for shamans in Neolithic Britain and Ireland is weak. The case rests mainly on iconography, monument architecture, and ethnographic analogy.

The classic altered-states model comes from **J. David Lewis-Williams** and **Thomas Dowson**, who argued that recurring geometric forms in prehistoric art can derive from **entoptic phenomena** seen in altered states of consciousness. For Atlantic megalithic art, **Richard Bradley** opened this discussion, and **Jeremy Dronfield** applied it most explicitly to Irish passage tombs such as **Knowth** and **Newgrange**, suggesting that spirals, lozenges, zigzags, and tunnel-like passage experience could reflect visionary states.

There is a real archaeological basis for taking the question seriously. Passage tomb art is repetitive, highly structured, and often tied to restricted architectural movement, darkness, illumination, and bodily experience. But the leap from altered states to “shamanism” is not secure. **Chris Fowler** has summarized the main objections: geometric motifs do not have to be entoptic, altered states do not require shamans, and “shamanism” is too broad a category to explain a specific sedentary farming society. That caution now represents the mainstream.

The evidence for psychoactive plants is even more fragile in Britain itself. The most famous claim involved **henbane** residues on Grooved Ware from the **Balfarg/Balbirnie** complex in Fife, but reanalysis by **Deborah Long** and colleagues found no evidence for henbane or other poisonous plants. There is no good archaeological case for **fly agaric** use in Neolithic Britain. For **opium poppy**, the situation is different but still limited. Work by **Aurelie Salavert** and colleagues shows secure Neolithic presence and dispersal of **Papaver somniferum** in western Europe, but this demonstrates cultivation and movement more clearly than ritual intoxication, and it does not provide direct proof for Britain specifically.

Mesolithic continuity is often brought into the discussion through **Star Carr** in North Yorkshire. The modified red deer antler frontlets from the early Mesolithic have been interpreted by **Chantal Conneller** in terms of bodily transformation and by **Aimee Little** and colleagues as possible “shamanic costume.” These are serious interpretations, but Star Carr lies thousands of years before Stonehenge. It offers a deep-time precedent for ritualized transformation, not a straight line of continuity into the Neolithic.

The most responsible conclusion is therefore narrow: Neolithic Britain and nearby Atlantic regions show evidence for powerful ritual settings, restricted access, symbolic art, and perhaps altered-state practices. Calling the system “shamanic” remains a hypothesis, not a settled fact.

## 5. Recent Discoveries, 2020-2026

Recent discoveries have reshaped the study of Neolithic religion mainly by clarifying **scale**, **connectivity**, and **kinship**, not by revealing named deities or explicit belief statements.

In **July 2020**, **David Nash**, **T. Jake R. Ciborowski**, **Mike Parker Pearson**, **Tim Darvill**, and colleagues showed in *Science Advances* that most of Stonehenge's **sarsens** came from **West Woods** in Wiltshire, around **25 km** north of the site. This replaced a vague “local stone” model with a specific source landscape. In **August 2024**, **Anthony Clarke**, **Christopher Kirkland**, **Richard Bevins**, **Nick Pearce**, **Stijn Glorie**, and **Rob Ixer** traced the **Altar Stone** not to Wales but to the **Orcadian Basin in northeast Scotland**, implying transport of at least **750 km**. A 2024 synthesis by **Parker Pearson**, **Bradley**, **Bevins**, **Ixer**, **Pearce**, and **Colin Richards** pushed the implication further by linking the stone’s recumbent placement to the recumbent stone circle tradition of northeast Scotland, including the Aberdeenshire sphere. Stonehenge now looks less like an isolated Wessex sanctuary and more like a monument assembled from deliberately gathered distant materials.

The sacred landscape around Stonehenge has also grown larger. In **June 2020**, **Vincent Gaffney** and colleagues proposed that huge pits around **Durrington Walls** formed a circuit more than **2 km** across. In **2025**, follow-up work strengthened the case that at least **16 pits** were deliberate prehistoric features. Whatever their exact function, they imply a formal ceremonial boundary at a scale not previously recognized.

Other discoveries reinforce the visual and symbolic networks of the period. The **Burton Agnes drum**, announced in **February 2022** by **Neil Wilkin**, **Mark Allen**, and **Alice Beasley**, came from a child burial dated to **3005-2890 BCE**. It strengthens the case that carved chalk objects formed part of a shared symbolic vocabulary across Britain and perhaps Ireland at roughly the time of Stonehenge's earlier phases. In **January 2025**, **Phil Harding**, **David Nash**, **T. Jake R. Ciborowski**, and colleagues argued that the **Cuckoo Stone** and **Tor Stone** were also moved from West Woods and may have formed an earlier “portal” into the Stonehenge landscape. Approach and threshold now look more important than older site-centered models allowed.

Ancient DNA has been equally important. In late **2021** and print **2022**, **Chris Fowler**, **Inigo Olalde**, **Vicki Cummings**, **David Reich**, and colleagues reconstructed a five-generation pedigree from **Hazleton North** long cairn in Gloucestershire. They found strong **patrilineal descent** and patterned placement of maternal lines within the tomb. In **April 2026**, **Vicki Cummings**, **Chris Fowler**, **Iñigo Olalde**, **David Reich**, and colleagues extended this picture across tombs in **Caithness and Orkney**, describing “webs of descent.” The significance for religion is substantial: tombs were not passive repositories for the dead but active technologies for materializing ancestry, descent, and belonging across generations.

Even geology has affected religious interpretation. Work by **Bevins** and colleagues in **2025** and by **Clarke** and colleagues in **January 2026** further weakened glacial explanations for Stonehenge’s exotic stones. The balance of evidence now favors **intentional human transport**, which in turn strengthens ritual, ancestral-political, and island-wide connectivity models over accidental ones.

Taken together, recent work has made Neolithic religion in Britain look more networked and more monumental than before. The strongest advances concern **kinship**, **sacred boundaries**, and **long-distance movement of charged materials**.

## Conclusion

The religion of Neolithic Britain was not a single cult centered on Stonehenge. It was a set of practices distributed across fields, wetlands, tombs, monuments, animals, and carved stones. Farming reshaped religion by tying sacred time to sowing, herd management, winter feasting, and solstitial gatherings. Rivers and marshes became places of route-making and selective deposition. Rock art and passage-tomb carving created durable sacred surfaces. Altered-state interpretations remain possible but contested. Recent discoveries, especially between **2020 and 2026**, have made one larger point unmistakable: the sacred geography of late Neolithic Britain was island-scale. Materials, people, animals, and ancestral claims were being drawn together across extraordinary distances. Stonehenge was one node in that network, not the whole story.
